# DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments

Wenjie Tang1,2,†, Yuan Zhou2,†, Keyan Cheng2, Erqiang Xu2, Liquan Xiao1, Minne Li2,\*  
1College of Computer, National University of Defense Technology  
2Intelligent Game and Decision Lab (IGDL)  
\*Corresponding author  †Equal contribution

###### Abstract

Large language model (LLM)-based agents are increasingly applied to complex strategic environments that demand long-horizon reasoning, multi-agent interaction, and decision-making under uncertainty. However, common existing benchmarks either assess isolated skills, lack environmental diversity, or rely on broad overall metrics. To address these issues, we introduce DSGBench, a more rigorous evaluation platform for strategic decision-making tasks. Firstly, it incorporates six complex strategic games which serve as ideal testbeds due to their long-term and multi-dimensional decision-making demands and flexibility in customizing tasks with various difficulty levels and targets. Secondly, DSGBench employs a fine-grained evaluation scoring system which examines the decision-making capabilities by looking into the performance in five specific dimensions, offering a comprehensive assessment in a better-designed fashion. Furthermore, DSGBench also incorporates an automated decision-tracking mechanism which enables in-depth analysis of agent behaviour patterns and the turning points in their strategies. We evaluate six popular LLM agents, including open-source and closed-source models, and observe distinct strengths and limitations among various tasks. Through decision trajectory analysis, we further identify systemic limitations in different LLMs. These findings offer valuable insights for model selection and future LLM-based agent development.

### 1 Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated remarkable progress in reasoning, planning, and problem solving, particularly within well-defined, closed-world environments such as web navigation, assistant programming, and task execution in simulated settings (Lai et al., [2024](#bib.bib134 "AutoWebGLM: a large language model-based web navigating agent"); Jiang et al., [2024](#bib.bib135 "A survey on large language models for code generation"); Wang et al., [2024a](#bib.bib136 "Large language models for robotics: opportunities, challenges, and perspectives")). Motivated by these advancements, there is growing interest in extending the capabilities of LLMs to more complex and dynamic domains, including open-world scenarios and multi-agent strategic games (Wang et al., [2023](#bib.bib97 "Voyager: an open-ended embodied agent with large language models"); Schmidgall et al., [2025](#bib.bib142 "Agent laboratory: using llm agents as research assistants"); Hu et al., [2024](#bib.bib137 "A survey on large language model-based game agents")). Unlike closed settings, these environments are often partially observable, adversarial, and require long-horizon planning under uncertainty (He et al., [2024](#bib.bib138 "From words to actions: unveiling the theoretical underpinnings of llm-driven autonomous systems"); Park et al., [2023](#bib.bib107 "Generative agents: interactive simulacra of human behavior")). Applying LLMs in such contexts demands not only a deep understanding of other agents’ intentions and potentially deceptive behaviors, but also advanced reasoning to anticipate risks and weaknesses in evolving strategies (Zhang et al., [2024](#bib.bib12 "Agent-pro: learning to evolve via policy-level reflection and optimization"); Gandhi et al., [2023](#bib.bib139 "Strategic reasoning with language models")). Unlocking this broader potential calls for innovations in model architecture and the development of rigorous evaluation frameworks that can systematically assess their capabilities (Chang et al., [2024](#bib.bib112 "A survey on evaluation of large language models"); Liu et al., [2025](#bib.bib141 "Advances and challenges in foundation agents: from brain-inspired intelligence to evolutionary, collaborative, and safe systems")).

Figure 1: The overall framework of DSGBench. The framework consists of (1) a multi-game environment supporting both asynchronous and synchronous interactions; (2) fine-grained capability metrics for strategic planning, real-time decision-making, social reasoning, team collaboration, and adaptive learning; and (3) decision trajectory tracking tools that collaboratively analyze agents’ decision-making processes. Through observation-to-prompt and response-to-action loops, DSGBench enables systematic evaluation of LLM-based agents in dynamic, multi-agent scenarios.

Despite significant advances in LLM benchmarking, existing assessment frameworks suffer from three key limitations when applied to complicated decision-making settings. Firstly, agent performance in complex environments depends on the coordination of multiple interdependent cognitive abilities. However, existing benchmarks often target only single-dimensional skills and fail to provide a systematic evaluation of these integrated capabilities (Xie et al., [2024](#bib.bib92 "Travelplanner: a benchmark for real-world planning with language agents"); Zhong et al., [2023](#bib.bib106 "Agieval: a human-centric benchmark for evaluating foundation models")). Secondly, many benchmarks employ environments that are either overly simplistic or lack structural diversity, limiting their ability to evaluate model performance across varied and complex decision-making tasks (Duan et al., [2024](#bib.bib39 "Gtbench: uncovering the strategic reasoning limitations of llms via game-theoretic evaluations"); Costarelli et al., [2024](#bib.bib26 "GameBench: evaluating strategic reasoning abilities of llm agents")). Furthermore, common evaluation metrics focus extensively on result-oriented performance like task completion rate and win rate (Liu et al., [2023](#bib.bib42 "Agentbench: evaluating llms as agents"); Xi et al., [2024](#bib.bib23 "AgentGym: evolving large language model-based agents across diverse environments"); Wu et al., [2023](#bib.bib25 "Smartplay: a benchmark for llms as intelligent agents"); Xu et al., [2023](#bib.bib24 "Magic: investigation of large language model powered multi-agent in cognition, adaptability, rationality and collaboration")). However, most models have near-zero success rates in challenging environments (Xie et al., [2024](#bib.bib92 "Travelplanner: a benchmark for real-world planning with language agents")), and therefore, overly broad assessment scores are difficult to evaluate strengths and weaknesses among LLM-based agents properly.

To address these challenges, we introduce DSGBench, a diverse strategic game benchmark designed to assess LLM-based agents across multiple dimensions of cognitive and interactive capabilities. The overall framework of DSGBench is shown in Figure  [1](#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"). First of all, it includes six classic strategic games which are StarCraft II (Ma et al., [2023a](#bib.bib36 "Large language models play starcraft ii: benchmarks and a chain of summarization approach")), Civilization (Qi et al., [2024](#bib.bib38 "CivRealm: a learning and reasoning odyssey in civilization for decision-making agents")), Street Fighter III (Go et al., [2023](#bib.bib140 "A phase-change memristive reinforcement learning for rapidly outperforming champion street-fighter players")), Diplomacy (Mukobi et al., [2023](#bib.bib111 "Welfare diplomacy: benchmarking language model cooperation")), Werewolf (Bailis et al., [2024b](#bib.bib110 "Werewolf arena: a case study in llm evaluation via social deduction")), and Stratego (Perolat et al., [2022](#bib.bib37 "Mastering the game of stratego with model-free multiagent reinforcement learning")). These games replicate the dynamics of the real world through multi-agent interactions, long-context decision-making, and the achievement of various sub-goals, providing a varied scene setting that allows for a comprehensive assessment of agents’ adaptability and cognitive integration. Furthermore, DSGBench breaks down the overall decision-making capability into five sub-dimensions, including strategic planning, real-time decision-making, social reasoning, team collaboration, and adaptive learning, and customizes tasks with various difficulty levels in the previously mentioned environments as test cases. A more fine-grained quantitative scoring system is designed, which provides deeper insights into agent capabilities beyond conventional success rates. Last but not least, our benchmark integrates a detailed decision-tracking mechanism, offering researchers clear visibility into agents’ underlying decision-making processes. This transparency is essential for improving model interpretability and advancing agent design. We have conducted a systematic evaluation of six representative LLM-based agentsunder complex and variable-controlled setups. Results show that they exhibit varying performance and present systemic limitations under different types of tasks, rather than demonstrating consistent general competence.

### 2 Related Work

#### 2.1 LLM-based agents

LLM-based agents present human-like cognitive abilities to solve decision-making problems (Liu et al., [2025](#bib.bib141 "Advances and challenges in foundation agents: from brain-inspired intelligence to evolutionary, collaborative, and safe systems")). In early applications of the LLMs, they achieved significant success in handling relatively simple tasks such as role-playing and code generation (Nijkamp et al., [2022](#bib.bib116 "Codegen: an open large language model for code with multi-turn program synthesis"); Park et al., [2023](#bib.bib107 "Generative agents: interactive simulacra of human behavior")) due to their strong capabilities in instruction following, language comprehension, and generation (Wang et al., [2024c](#bib.bib117 "A survey on large language model based autonomous agents")). As the research progressed, researchers began to focus on how to interact LLMs with external tools or environments (Schick et al., [2023](#bib.bib118 "Toolformer: language models can teach themselves to use tools"); Tang et al., [2024](#bib.bib119 "Worldcoder, a model-based llm agent: building world models by writing code and interacting with the environment")), where the key technique is to provide relevant instruction and environment information through context, which enables LLM-based agents to generate executable actions in response to complex tasks. In addition, researchers have systematically explored the capabilities of LLM-based agents in perception, memory, decision-making, and tool use, with application areas ranging from web navigation, software development, and game decision-making (Wang et al., [2024c](#bib.bib117 "A survey on large language model based autonomous agents"); Zhou et al., [2023](#bib.bib13 "WebArena: a realistic web environment for building autonomous agents"); Qian et al., [2024](#bib.bib19 "Chatdev: communicative agents for software development"); Ma et al., [2023b](#bib.bib109 "Large language models play starcraft ii: benchmarks and a chain of summarization approach")). Among these advances, the emergent reasoning capabilities of LLM-based agents are particularly critical and are an integral part of the decision-making process (Hao et al., [2024](#bib.bib120 "LLM reasoners: new evaluation, library, and analysis of step-by-step reasoning with large language models")). Furthermore, it is found that LLMs have demonstrated advanced cognitive capabilities such as human-like chain-of-thought reasoning, world modeling, and theory of mind (Wei et al., [2022](#bib.bib64 "Chain-of-thought prompting elicits reasoning in large language models"); Hao et al., [2023](#bib.bib31 "Reasoning with language model is planning with world model"); Shapira et al., [2023](#bib.bib121 "Clever hans or neural theory of mind? stress testing social reasoning in large language models")). These capabilities influence aspects of how LLM-based agents respond to changes in adversary strategies, how they make rational decisions in uncertain environments, and how they maintain long-term and short-term consistency across successive decision-making processes.

#### 2.2 Existing Benchmarks for LLM-based agents

With the enhancement of the comprehensive capabilities of LLMs, there is a growing trend to evaluate their performance in more challenging open-world or complex gaming scenarios (Hu et al., [2025](#bib.bib143 "A survey on large language model-based game agents")). Although static datasets provided effective evaluation in early studies, they failed to reflect the true performance of LLMs in dynamic and complex environments  (Huang et al., [2024](#bib.bib94 "C-eval: a multi-level multi-discipline chinese evaluation suite for foundation models"); Bai et al., [2023](#bib.bib122 "Longbench: a bilingual, multitask benchmark for long context understanding")). To address this limitation, subsequent research has begun to evaluate LLM-based agents in executable environments, focusing on revealing their capabilities in an open-ended generation, multi-round interaction, and agent-based role-playing (Wang et al., [2024c](#bib.bib117 "A survey on large language model based autonomous agents")). Current researchers have focused on areas such as software development, embodied agents, society simulations, gaming, and policy making (Hong et al., [2023](#bib.bib123 "Metagpt: meta programming for multi-agent collaborative framework"); Mandi et al., [2024](#bib.bib124 "Roco: dialectic multi-robot collaboration with large language models"); Kaiya et al., [2023](#bib.bib125 "Lyfe agents: generative agents for low-cost real-time social interactions"); Mukobi et al., [2023](#bib.bib111 "Welfare diplomacy: benchmarking language model cooperation")), which have provided LLM assessments with more authentic and diverse contexts.

In particular, strategy games have been widely recognized as ideal experimental platforms for evaluating decision-making capabilities of LLM-based agents due to their complex reasoning requirements and dynamic interaction properties  (Liu et al., [2023](#bib.bib42 "Agentbench: evaluating llms as agents"); Hu et al., [2025](#bib.bib143 "A survey on large language model-based game agents")). They require agents to engage in multistep reasoning, predict the actions of other agents, and balance competing goals under uncertainty, thus reflecting the complex decision-making processes encountered in real-world scenarios (Huang et al., [2025](#bib.bib144 "How far are we on the decision-making of llms? evaluating llms’ gaming ability in multi-agent environments")). However, as shown in table [1](#S2.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 2.1 LLM-based agents ‣ 2 Related Work ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), benchmarks such as GTBench (Duan et al., [2024](#bib.bib39 "Gtbench: uncovering the strategic reasoning limitations of llms via game-theoretic evaluations")) and SmartPlay (Xi et al., [2024](#bib.bib23 "AgentGym: evolving large language model-based agents across diverse environments")) focus on unidimensional capabilities, neglecting the integrated cognitive skills required to cope with interdependence and uncertainty in dynamic environments. In addition, benchmarks such as AgentBench (Liu et al., [2023](#bib.bib42 "Agentbench: evaluating llms as agents")) and GameBench (Costarelli et al., [2024](#bib.bib26 "GameBench: evaluating strategic reasoning abilities of llm agents")) use eventual success as the core metric, which limits insight into complex decision-making processes.

### 3 DSGBench - Overview

DSGBench is a benchmark for evaluating the strategic decision-making capabilities of LLM-based agents through a diverse suite of strategy games. It consists of three key components: (i) a suite of complex strategy games covering various interaction settings, (ii) fine-grained evaluation metrics designed to capture different aspects of decision-making capabilities, and (iii) decision-tracking mechanisms for structured analysis of agent behavior over time. DSGBench adopts a unified Gym-based interface to standardize agent-environment interactions across tasks. This interface facilitates configurable tasks and supports the integration of new games and extension of existing ones. Additionally, the framework incorporates an automated scoring process that permits the customisation of scoring tasks as required. #### 3.1 Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities

Effective decision-making in complex environments involves the coordination of multiple cognitive processes. According to Dual Systems Cognitive Theory  (Kahneman, [2011](#bib.bib128 "Thinking, fast and slow")), rational planning and analytical thinking rely on "System 2", while rapid decision-making is accomplished through the intuitive responses of "System 1". The complementarity of the two allows individuals to make quick decisions and maintain certain goals in dynamic and uncertain environments. However, in complex situations involving multiple intelligences, the ability to make decisions collectively, and individual capabilities alone are often insufficient. Distributed Cognition Theory emphasizes that the ability to reason socially and work in a team stems from the interaction of the individual with the environment, tools, and other subjects (Hutchins, [1995](#bib.bib129 "Cognition in the wild")). Furthermore, Dynamic Decision Theory states that, in dynamic environments, individuals and groups can adapt their decision-making strategies in response to feedback, thereby exhibiting adaptive behaviours in response to changing external conditions (Edwards, [1962](#bib.bib130 "Dynamic decision theory and probabilistic information processings")).

Drawing inspiration from human cognitive decision-making theories, DSGBench adopts a five-dimensional framework for evaluating LLM-based agents, as illustrated in Table [2](#S3.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"): (i) strategic planning (deep analysis via System 2), (ii) real-time decision-making (fast, intuitive responses via System 1), (iii) social reasoning (distributed information processing), (iv) team collaboration (multi-agent coordination), and (v) adaptive learning (feedback-driven strategy adaptation). A detailed breakdown of these dimensions is provided in Appendix [A.1](#A1.SS1 "A.1 Key Dimensions of Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities ‣ Appendix A Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

#### 3.2 Diverse Strategic Environments and Scenarios

To effectively evaluate cognitive decision-making capabilities of LLM-based agents, we curated a diverse set of strategic games. As summarized in Table [2](#S3.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), each core capability is mapped to a representative subset of games specifically designed to target that dimension. The selection was guided by key criteria, including decision-making demands, gameplay mechanics, and overall difficulty. These games collectively present long-horizon, multidimensional challenges that test agents across five core dimensions of cognitive decision-making capabilities. The following sections explain the specific challenges posed by the games chosen to evaluate these dimensions.

As demonstrated in Table  [3](#S3.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 3.1 Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), a set of strategic games was selected to ensure that each capability is adequately evaluated, with factors such as game mechanics, difficulty, and other key aspects being considered. These games present long-term, multidimensional decision-making challenges and also assess five key dimensions of cognitive decision-making capability through multifaceted evaluation perspectives. The following sections explain the specific challenges posed by the games chosen to evaluate these dimensions.

StarCraft II (Ma et al., [2023b](#bib.bib109 "Large language models play starcraft ii: benchmarks and a chain of summarization approach")) is a complex real-time strategy (RTS) game where players build bases, manage resources, raise armies, and destroy enemy bases. LLM-based agents must make efficient decisions, optimize resource management, engage in strategic planning, and adapt to their opponents’ tactics in real-time within a rapidly changing and high-pressure environment.

Civilization (Qi et al., [2024](#bib.bib38 "CivRealm: a learning and reasoning odyssey in civilization for decision-making agents")) is a turn-based strategy game where players lead a civilization from ancient times to the future. The game involves city-building, resource management, technological development, cultural growth, and diplomacy, with the goal of creating a strong, prosperous civilization. LLM-based agents must make long-term decisions, wisely allocate resources, plan future development, and engage in complex diplomatic negotiations.

Street Fighter III (Street et al., [2024](#bib.bib28 "LLMs achieve adult human performance on higher-order theory of mind tasks")) is a fast-paced fighting game where players control characters with unique skills and combos to battle each other. LLM-based agents must make quick decisions, execute precise combos, and anticipate and counter their opponents’ moves in a high-pressure environment.

Diplomacy ((FAIR)† et al., [2022](#bib.bib35 "Human-level play in the game of diplomacy by combining language models with strategic reasoning")) is a multiplayer strategy board game where players expand their territory through negotiations, alliances, and betrayals. Each player controls a country, and the goal is to gain an advantage through strategic positioning and diplomatic agreements. LLM-based agents must build alliances, manage complex diplomatic relationships, and predict opponents’ actions.

Werewolf (Bailis et al., [2024a](#bib.bib34 "Werewolf arena: a case study in llm evaluation via social deduction")) is a social reasoning-based multiplayer game where players are secretly assigned roles, with some being werewolves and others villagers. The werewolves aim to destroy the villagers, while the villagers must identify the werewolves. LLM-based agents need to make decisions with limited information, assess the credibility of others, and adjust their strategies based on changing social dynamics.

Stratego (Perolat et al., [2022](#bib.bib37 "Mastering the game of stratego with model-free multiagent reinforcement learning")) is a strategic board game where players move pieces on a board to capture the opponent’s flag. The game emphasizes planning, bluffing, and reasoning about the opponent’s strategy. LLM-based agents must make decisions with incomplete information, predict their opponents’ actions, and conceal their own plans.

Customizable Evaluation Tasks. Despite the fact that the various strategic games employed in DSGBench encompass a broad range of decision-making capabilities, the fixed game settings may not adequately assess an agent’s multi-dimensional capabilities performance in dynamic environments. Consequently, DSGBench offers the flexibility to create customized evaluation tasks through scenario variables, enabling targeted assessments based on specific needs. As shown in Table [3](#S3.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 3.1 Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), it provides precise control over various scenario variables, such as opponent behavior patterns, interaction paradigms (e.g., synchronous vs. asynchronous), prompt engineering approaches, and reasoning strategies. Additionally, the extensive interaction trajectories across games, ranging from 24 iterations in Street Fighter III to 1270 in Stratego, challenge LLM-based agents, requiring advanced capabilities in contextual learning, long-term strategic planning, and decision consistency. For a detailed description of the game mechanics, aciton space and other information, please refer to the Appendix [E](#A5 "Appendix E StarCraft II ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

#### 3.3 Fine-Grained Capability Metrics

In order to address the limitations of traditional capability assessment methods in dynamic and complex environments, a fine-grained capability metric is introduced. The multiple metrics is motivated by two empirical findings: (1) outcome-based metrics are difficult to capture detailed differences between capability dimensions, and (2) gamification assessment requires the establishment of interpretable measurement benchmarks. The metrics in DSGBench are defined based on expert insights into the core mechanics of each game, ensuring alignment with the key competencies being assessed. Specifically, each metric is chosen to reflect a critical aspect that influences strategic decision-making performance. As illustrated in Table [2](#S3.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), the proposed methodology first establishes a mapping relationship between capability dimensions and games at the macro level, and then associates each game with a set of fine-grained metrics at the micro level. More details of each metric can be found in the Metrics subsection in Appendix [E](#A5 "Appendix E StarCraft II ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

##### An example.

We take evaluating the Strategic Planning capability in the game StarCraft II as an example. Efficient resource management, which involves collecting and allocating minerals and gases, allows players to sustain their forces, while supply utilization governs their capacity to deploy units effectively. Based on these mechanics, fine-grained metrics such as resource management efficiency and supply utilization are key for assessing a player’s strategic planning. As a complement to the quantitative evaluation, DSGBench introduces a decision-tracking and behavioural analysis system that combines performance metrics with contextual analysis to deepen the understanding of LLM decision-making. The system captures key decision points throughout the game and links them to real-time game states and mission objectives, thereby revealing underlying strategic reasoning patterns. Specifically, the analytical framework consists of three core components: (1) action types that categorize specific decisions, such as resource allocation and unit production; (2) decision contexts that capture the game state and objectives at each decision point; and (3) outcomes that assess the impact of decisions on game progression.

We employ a two-layered action classification approach that infers decisions from the game API, enhanced by domain-specific heuristics. For each decision, DSGBench records observable game states, including unit configurations, resource reserves, and historical events, along with opponent actions within a sliding window. Intermediate goals inferred from the LLM’s chain-of-thought outputs are also captured, enabling rapid identification of critical decision points and issues. This approach reveals the decision-making logic of LLM-based agents and lays the foundation for further optimisation. As shown in Figure [2](#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.4 Decision Trajectory Tracking ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments") and Table [4](#S3.T4 "Table 4 ‣ 3.4 Decision Trajectory Tracking ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), these insights offer a more precise understanding of the evolution of LLM decision-making. ### 4 Experiments

This section presents the results of the evaluation of the six representative LLMs on DSGBench. The following subsections describe the evaluation setup, the main experimental results, and both quantitative and qualitative results that highlight the strengths and limitations of the models.

#### 4.1 Evaluation Setup

We evaluated six representative LLMs on DSGBench, including closed-source models (GPT-4o(OpenAI, [2024](#bib.bib57 "Hello gpt-4o")), GPT-3.5-Turbo(OpenAI, [2023](#bib.bib5 "GPT-3.5 turbo")), Gemini 1.5 Flash(Reid et al., [2024](#bib.bib4 "Gemini 1.5: unlocking multimodal understanding across millions of tokens of context"))) and open-source models (DeepSeek-V2.5(DeepSeek-AI, [2024](#bib.bib3 "DeepSeek-v2: a strong, economical, and efficient mixture-of-experts language model")), Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct(Dubey et al., [2024](#bib.bib6 "The llama 3 herd of models"))). All models were assessed using standardized prompts without fine-tuning across six game environments. For prompt engineering, we adopted existing prompt templates for established game environments (StarCraft II(Ma et al., [2023b](#bib.bib109 "Large language models play starcraft ii: benchmarks and a chain of summarization approach")), Civilization(Qi et al., [2024](#bib.bib38 "CivRealm: a learning and reasoning odyssey in civilization for decision-making agents")), Diplomacy(Mukobi et al., [2023](#bib.bib111 "Welfare diplomacy: benchmarking language model cooperation")), Werewolf(Bailis et al., [2024a](#bib.bib34 "Werewolf arena: a case study in llm evaluation via social deduction")), and Street Fighter III(Wikipedia, [2024b](#bib.bib48 "Street fighter iii"))), while developing custom prompt structures and reasoning frameworks for Stratego(Wikipedia, [2024a](#bib.bib53 "Stratego")). To ensure consistency and fairness, each game was interfaced through a unified text-based interaction loop, as detailed in Appendix [B.2](#A2.SS2 "B.2 Game Interaction Formulation ‣ Appendix B Overview of DSGBench Implementation ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), and models competed in 10 matches per scenario against either built-in AIs or GPT-4o-mini. The temperature was fixed at 0.2 to balance determinism and reasoning.

As demonstrated in Table [3](#S3.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 3.1 Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments"), our benchmark facilitates precise control over a range of scenario variables, including opponent behaviour patterns, interaction paradigms (e.g., synchronous vs. asynchronous), prompt engineering approaches, and reasoning strategies. These variables can be customised to align with the distinct characteristics inherent in each game type. Further details on scenario configurations, prompt engineering, and evaluation metrics are provided in Appendix  [E](#A5 "Appendix E StarCraft II ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

#### 4.2 Quantitative Results

Fine-grained capability metrics reveal distinct patterns in model performance across different cognitive dimensions. The comprehensive evaluation framework, encompassing strategic planning, real-time decision-making, social reasoning, team collaboration, and adaptive learning, demonstrates that models exhibit specialised strengths rather than uniform capabilities. This specialised performance is particularly evident in the case of Gemini 1.5 Flash (Reid et al., [2024](#bib.bib4 "Gemini 1.5: unlocking multimodal understanding across millions of tokens of context")), which achieves exceptional results in strategic planning (72.88) and adaptive learning (64.23), while showing limitations in real-time decision-making (48.45). In contrast, GPT-4o (OpenAI, [2024](#bib.bib57 "Hello gpt-4o")) exhibits a more balanced set of capabilities across various metrics. It demonstrates a particularly strong performance in social reasoning (83.27) and team collaboration (34.31), along with consistent scores above 40 in other dimensions.

The performance analysis reveals a substantial capability gap between closed-source and open-source models. Closed-source models have been shown to demonstrate superior performance, with Gemini 1.5 Flash (Reid et al., [2024](#bib.bib4 "Gemini 1.5: unlocking multimodal understanding across millions of tokens of context")) and GPT-4o (OpenAI, [2024](#bib.bib57 "Hello gpt-4o")) achieving overall scores of 56.16 and 54.10, respectively. In contrast, open-source alternatives such as DeepSeek-V2.5(DeepSeek-AI, [2024](#bib.bib3 "DeepSeek-v2: a strong, economical, and efficient mixture-of-experts language model")) and Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., [2024](#bib.bib6 "The llama 3 herd of models")) achieve significantly lower overall scores of 53.75 and 45.11, respectively. The most pronounced disparity is observed in strategic planning tasks, where Gemini 1.5 Flash(Reid et al., [2024](#bib.bib4 "Gemini 1.5: unlocking multimodal understanding across millions of tokens of context")) (72.88) significantly outperforms Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct(Dubey et al., [2024](#bib.bib6 "The llama 3 herd of models")) (51.47). However, in specific scenarios such as StarCraft’s real-time decision-making, open-source models can achieve competitive performance, as demonstrated by Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct’s(Dubey et al., [2024](#bib.bib6 "The llama 3 herd of models")) high score in that dimension.

Game-specific analysis further illuminates the relationship between model architecture and task performance. In strategic games such as Civilization, closed-source models demonstrate clear advantages, with Gemini 1.5 Flash(Reid et al., [2024](#bib.bib4 "Gemini 1.5: unlocking multimodal understanding across millions of tokens of context")) achieving a high score of 72.88 in strategic planning. Conversely, GPT-3.5 Turbo(OpenAI, [2023](#bib.bib5 "GPT-3.5 turbo")) exhibits diminished efficacy in this domain, attaining a score of 32.94. In contrast, in real-time gaming environments, where the speed of decision-making is paramount, models such as Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct demonstrate superior performance with a score of 66.35 in real-time decision-making. The observed variance in performance across different game types suggests that current Agent architectures may be optimised for specific cognitive tasks, potentially at the expense of others.

#### 4.3 Qualitative Analysis

Our comprehensive analysis of decision trajectories generated by LLM-based agents across the diverse suite of games within DSGBench has revealed several recurrent, systemic limitations in their decision-making capabilities. These limitations, while varying in manifestation across different game genres and complexities, point to fundamental challenges in current LLM architectures when applied to strategic environments. We briefly outline three primary limitations here, with detailed empirical evidence and case studies provided in  [D](#A4 "Appendix D Decision Trajectory Analysis: Examples and Insights ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

Difficulty balancing long-term planning with immediate decision-making. LLM-based agents set long-term goals such as economic growth and technological advancement in games like StarCraft II. However, they often struggle to adapt to immediate tactical needs, sticking to predetermined strategies while missing opportunities to respond to emerging threats or capitalize on fleeting advantages. This reveals a challenge in integrating long-term goals with dynamic, short-term demands. Further analysis of this issue is provided in [3.4](#S3.SS4 "3.4 Decision Trajectory Tracking ‣ 3 DSGBench - Overview ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments") and [D.1](#A4.SS1 "D.1 Case 1: Difficulty balancing long-term planning with immediate decision-making. ‣ Appendix D Decision Trajectory Analysis: Examples and Insights ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

Challenges handling incomplete information and multi-hop inference, especially in games like Stratego. In these environments, decision-making depends on inferring hidden pieces and predicting future moves. LLM-based agents frequently make decisions based on incomplete inferences, often failing to revise their beliefs when new information surfaces. This limits their ability to make strategic decisions in situations requiring predictions from partial data. Further analysis of this issue is provided in [D.2](#A4.SS2 "D.2 Case 2: Challenges handling incomplete information and multi-hop inference. ‣ Appendix D Decision Trajectory Analysis: Examples and Insights ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

Difficulty overcoming memory limitations in long horizon tasks. In social deduction games like Diplomacy, success relies on remembering past interactions, understanding other agents’ intentions, and adapting strategies. LLM-based agents often fail to maintain a consistent memory of key events, leading to inconsistent decisions and repetitive strategies that can be exploited by more adaptive opponents. Further analysis of this issue is provided in [D.3](#A4.SS3 "D.3 Case 3: Difficulty overcoming memory limitations in long horizon tasks. ‣ Appendix D Decision Trajectory Analysis: Examples and Insights ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

### 5 Conclusion

We introduce DSGBench, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate the strategic decision-making capabilities of LLM-based agents in diverse and dynamic gaming environments. In this paper, we assess LLM-based agents based on key cognitive decision-making dimensions from human cognition and propose an integrated evaluation approach. Under standardized settings, we systematically evaluate the performance of six representative LLM-based agents in complex strategic environments. Through fine-grained evaluation metrics and decision trajectory analysis, we reveal the strengths and weaknesses of agents in various scenarios. Experimental results show significant differences across multiple ability dimensions. Additionally, we have established a unified evaluation framework that supports the integration of new games and the customization and expansion of new game scenarios. DSGBench enables us to select the most suitable agents for specific tasks or those with strengths in certain capabilities. We hope that DSGBench will see widespread application, as strategic gaming itself is an evolving process. Agents can continuously learn and evolve through interaction with opponents, making game-based evaluation methods virtually limitless in potential.

### Appendix A Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities

Cognitive decision-making capabilities are essential for effective problem-solving and achieving goals in dynamic environments. These capabilities involve the ability to plan strategically, make real-time decisions under pressure, collaborate in teams, and learn from experience. The following are key dimensions of cognitive decision-making capabilities:

#### A.1 Key Dimensions of Cognitive Decision-Making Capabilities

Strategic Planning \[Kahneman, [2011](#bib.bib128 "Thinking, fast and slow")\] refers to the ability to formulate and implement long-term strategies that are consistent with overall goals. This ability includes optimizing resources, anticipating future scenarios, and adapting to changing environments.

Real-Time Decision-Making \[Kahneman, [2011](#bib.bib128 "Thinking, fast and slow")\] refers to the ability to make effective decisions under time pressure. It involves managing competing objectives, processing dynamic information, and reacting quickly to unpredictable changes.

Social Reasoning \[Hutchins, [1995](#bib.bib129 "Cognition in the wild")\] refers to the ability to understand and navigate interactions in a team or competitive environment. This ability requires understanding the intentions of other agents, predicting their behaviour, and adjusting strategies accordingly.

Team Collaboration \[Hutchins, [1995](#bib.bib129 "Cognition in the wild")\] refers to the ability of agents to work together effectively in a multi-agent environment to achieve a common goal. This includes coordinating actions, communicating intentions, and solving collective problems.

Adaptive Learning \[Edwards, [1962](#bib.bib130 "Dynamic decision theory and probabilistic information processings")\] refers to the ability of an agent to continuously improve its capabilities by learning from past experiences and feedback. This ability includes identifying patterns, refining strategies, and adapting behaviours to incorporate new information into subsequent iterations, and the decision-making process is continuously optimized for continued success.

### Appendix B Overview of DSGBench Implementation

#### B.1 Overall Architecture

The proposed evaluation framework introduces an automated architecture for systematically evaluating LLM-based agents in various game environments. A unified gym interface is implemented for all games, which facilitates subsequent game extensions. In addition, the framework provides easy scenario customization to tailor the evaluation requirements based on a single profile.

The key component of the framework is the GameManager, which coordinates the evaluation process’s initialization and execution phases. The architecture consists of three main modules: DataCollector, GameEnv, and HistoryTracker. The DataCollector module handles the game and agent configurations through parameter parsing and establishes the base parameters for the evaluation. The GameEnv module defines the action and observation space and facilitates the interaction between agents and the evaluation through a standardized interface. The GameEnv module defines the action and observation space while facilitating the interaction between the agents and the environment through a standardized interface. The HistoryTracker module comprehensively records the game progress, agent model, and match-specific information.

During initialization, the GameManager coordinates component configuration through calls to GameEnv(args) and Agent(args), followed by prompt processing through Prompt(args) and LLM(args). The execution phase manages state transitions via env.step(action), facilitates agent decisions via agent.action(obs), and records state information via HistoryTracker. The framework supports a wide range of game genres, including real-time strategy games (StarCraft II), turn-based strategy games (Civilization), fighting strategy games (Street Fighter III), and social strategy games (Werewolf, Diplomacy). This enables a comprehensive evaluation in diverse decision-making environments.

#### B.2 Game Interaction Formulation

In DSGBench evaluation, we model agent-environment interactions as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP), formalized as the quintuple ⟨W,S,A,O,T⟩. Here, W denotes the victory condition—the strategic objectives agents must achieve, such as destroying the opponent’s base in StarCraft II or achieving victory through multiple pathways (combat conquest, cultural dominance, or scientific advancement) in Civilization. S represents the state space, encompassing all observable environmental states within the current game. A defines the action space of all legal actions available to an agent per time step, including movement, combat, dialogue, and negotiation. O comprises the observation space, containing environmental feedback ft that reflects immediate state changes and responses to agent actions. T represents the state transition function S×A→S, mapping how the current state st and agent action at determine the subsequent state st+1. In the context of DSGBench, the agents policy is implicitly the LLM’s reasoning process, mapping the history of observations (state st, action at−1, feedback ft−1) and the current observation ot to the next action at generated by LLMs, typically mediated via prompting. The formalizations for single-level and two-level inference model how agents make decisions at different levels of abstraction based on available information, as illustrated in Figure [4](#A2.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ B.2 Game Interaction Formulation ‣ Appendix B Overview of DSGBench Implementation ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

Figure 4: Formalization of agent-environment interaction in DSGBench. The diagram illustrates (top) single-level inference for centralized state-space games and (bottom) two-level inference, featuring micro and macro decision levels tailored for complex strategy games.

Single-level Inference. Games such as Street Fighter III, Diplomacy, Werewolf, and Stratego are particularly suited for single-level reasoning due to their centralized state and action spaces where a single policy can directly map states to actions. The reasoning process is formalized as

pπ​(τ)\=p​(s0)​∏t\=0T−1p​(at|st,ft)⋅T​(st+1|st,at)

(4)

where pπ​(τ) represents the policy trajectory, p​(s0) is the initial state distribution, p​(at|st,ft) denotes the probability of taking action at given state st and available feedback ft, and T​(st+1|st,at) is the state transition function, defining the probability of transitioning to state st+1 given the current state st and action at. In single-level reasoning games, agents can effectively accomplish complex tasks with simplified state and action reasoning operating at a single layer of abstraction.

Two-level Inference. Complex strategy games like StarCraft II and Civilization require agents to handle large observation spaces and multi-dimensional tasks through two levels of reasoning: macro-level strategic planning (for example, resource management and army deployment) and micro-level tactical decisions (for example, executing micro-operations and managing local combat). The reasoning process for two-level inference is formalized as:

pπ​(τ)

\=p​(s0)​∏t\=0T−1p​(atMicro∣st,ftMicro)⋅p​(atMacro∣st,atMicro,ftMacro)⋅T​(st+1∣st,atMicro,atMacro)

(5)

Here, atMicro represents immediate tactical actions based on local feedback ftMicro; atMacro denotes macro-level strategic decisions guided by the micro-level decisions atMicro and global feedback ftMacro, and T​(st+1∣st,atMicro,atMacro) defines the state transition function, reflecting environmental changes due to both micro atMicro and macro atMacro actions taken in state st.

### Appendix C Details of Experimental Evaluation

#### C.1 Automated Evaluation in DSGBench

The evaluation framework is an automated and simplified platform designed to evaluate LLM-based agents uniformly. The main component of the framework is the GameManager, which coordinates the initialization and execution phases. It is responsible for configuring the environment and the agent, ensuring a smooth game flow and accurately tracking the decision trajectory. The process starts with the DataCollector, which is responsible for collecting the basic configuration of the game and the agent, laying the foundation for accurate evaluation. This data is then fed into the modules GameEnv and HistoryTracker. While the former manages the action and observation space and enables seamless interaction between the agent and its environment, the latter captures the detailed game history and allows for in-depth analysis of the decision-making process and strategic choices. This setup provides a comprehensive view of the performance of LLM-based agents and helps to gain a deeper understanding of their behaviour and how the strategy of the agent evolves throughout the game. For a detailed description of the architectural design, please refer to Appendix [B.1](#A2.SS1 "B.1 Overall Architecture ‣ Appendix B Overview of DSGBench Implementation ‣ Supplementary Material ‣ DSGBench: A Diverse Strategic Game Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Agents in Complex Decision-Making Environments").

#### C.2 Customizable Evaluation Scenarios

Despite the fact that the various strategic games employed in DSGBench encompass a broad range of decision-making capabilities, the fixed game settings may not adequately assess an agent’s multi-dimensional capabilities performance in dynamic environments. #### D.3 Case 3: Difficulty overcoming memory limitations in long horizon tasks.

In Diplomacy, agents must adapt their strategies to changing alliances, threats, and information. Effective memory of past events and choices is crucial for such adaptation. This difficulty may stem from memory limitations or over-reliance on previously successful approaches. This case study examines how these issues can lead to repetitive actions.

In W1901A, Turkey made a non-aggression pact with Austria and said it had "positive communication" with Italy and Russia. ### Appendix E StarCraft II

StarCraft II is a real-time strategy (RTS) game developed by Blizzard Entertainment. It is the successor to StarCraft: Broodwar, one of the most successful RTS games. The objective of StarCraft II is to establish bases, manage resources, build armies, and destroy the enemy’s base. The most common game setup is 1v1, where each player chooses one of three races: Terran, Protoss, or Zerg. Each race has unique units, buildings, and mechanics, which result in different strategic approaches. Players start with a small base and a few units, and they gather resources to build additional units and structures. Additionally, players scout their opponent and develop new tactics. Numerous resources are available for learning StarCraft II, including Battle.net, TeamLiquid, and Wikia.

As a highly complex RTS game, StarCraft II provides an ideal environment for evaluating LLM-based agents. Its complexity stems not only from a vast action space and diverse unit types with unique abilities, but also from the critical interplay of micromanagement and macromanagement, all unfolding under conditions of imperfect information and severe time constraints. The game demands quick decision-making, efficient resource management, strategic planning, and the ability to adapt to the opponent’s tactics in real-time. LLM-based agents must make effective decisions in high-pressure, rapidly changing environments while also adjusting strategies over the long term.

StarCraft II has become a critical platform for reinforcement learning (RL) research\[Vinyals et al., [2017](#bib.bib131 "Starcraft ii: a new challenge for reinforcement learning")\]. DeepMind’s AlphaStar\[Vinyals et al., [2019](#bib.bib87 "Grandmaster level in starcraft ii using multi-agent reinforcement learning")\], for example, used deep reinforcement learning to defeat several professional players, demonstrating the potential of RL in dynamic, complex environments, particularly in real-time decision-making and long-term strategic planning. However, AlphaStar’s limitations in decision-making transparency have opened up further avenues for research.

With the rise of LLMs such as the GPT series\[OpenAI, [2023](#bib.bib5 "GPT-3.5 turbo"), [2024](#bib.bib57 "Hello gpt-4o")\], researchers have started exploring the application of LLMs to StarCraft II. However, the rapid decision-making required in RTS games presents significant challenges for directly applying LLMs. To address this, the research team\[Ma et al., [2023b](#bib.bib109 "Large language models play starcraft ii: benchmarks and a chain of summarization approach")\] developed the TextStarCraft II environment and introduced the Chain of Summarization (CoS) method, which successfully defeated the LV5 built-in AI. Furthermore, the SwarmBrain system\[Shao et al., [2024](#bib.bib132 "SwarmBrain: embodied agent for real-time strategy game starcraft ii via large language models")\], which combines the Overmind intelligent matrix and Swarm ReflexNet, has further enhanced the performance of LLMs in strategic decision-making.

### Appendix F Civilization

Civilization is a turn-based strategy game where players lead a civilization from ancient times to the future. The primary gameplay involves the "4X" elements: eXplore, eXpand, eXploit, and eXterminate. Players manage cities, resources, technological advancements, culture, and diplomacy. The main goal is to build a powerful civilization and dominate others through strategic conquest, cultural influence, technological superiority, or diplomatic alliances. To achieve victory, players must make strategic decisions over thousands of years, requiring long-term planning and careful execution.

The most common game setup is for players to control a civilization, typically in single-player or multiplayer modes. Each civilization has unique abilities, units, and bonuses, offering different strategic approaches. Players start with a small settlement and must expand by exploring the map, establishing new cities, gathering resources, and developing new technologies. As the game progresses, players engage in diplomacy with other civilizations, form alliances, wage wars, or try to surpass opponents in technology and culture. Decisions made, from strategic actions to political alliances, have long-term consequences that span hundreds of turns. Players can consult a variety of resources, such as the Civilization Wiki, to develop their strategic understanding of the game.

As a highly complex turn-based strategy game, Civilization offers a rich environment for evaluating LLM-based agents. The game requires agents to make long-term decisions, manage multiple resources, plan for future development, and engage in diplomatic negotiations.

CivRealm \[Qi et al., [2024](#bib.bib38 "CivRealm: a learning and reasoning odyssey in civilization for decision-making agents")\] and CivSim \[Wang et al., [2024b](#bib.bib133 "Digital player: evaluating large language models based human-like agent in games")\] are decision-making environments based on the Unciv game, in which civilizations are led by agents who make decisions in resource management, diplomacy, and warfare. CivRealm is characterized by dynamic, evolving scenarios with incomplete information and supports both reinforcement learning (RL) and LLMs, requiring agents to integrate learning and reasoning for effective decision-making.

The following are the metrics and their corresponding formulas for Civilization. These fine-grained metrics in DSGBench are designed based on expert insights into the core mechanics of the game, with each metric targeting a specific aspect of decision-making that directly influences the performance of LLM-based agents in Civilization.

*   •
    
    EGR (Economy Growth Rate): Measures the rate at which a player’s economy is growing over time.
    
    EGR\=Δ​ET
    
    where Δ​E represents the change in economic strength (e.g., income, total resources) over the period, and T is the duration of the period in minutes (or game turns).
    
*   •
    
    CER (City Establishment Rate): Quantifies the pace at which a player establishes new cities.
    
    CER\=Ncity,newT
    
    where Ncity,new is the number of new cities established during the period, and T is the duration of the period in minutes (or game turns).
    
*   •
    
    TRP (Technology Research Progress): Measures the advancement of a player’s technological development relative to the total available technologies.
    
    TRP\=(Ntech,resNtech,total)×100%
    
    where Ntech,res is the number of technologies researched by the player, and Ntech,total is the total number of unique technologies available in the game (or scenario).
    
*   •

    LUR\=(AsettledAland,total)×100%

*   •
    
    MGR (Military Growth Rate): Captures the speed at which a player’s military forces are expanding.
    
    MGR\=Δ​MunitsT
    
    where Δ​Munits is the net change in the number of military units (or military strength/power, if defined) during the period, and T is the duration of the period in minutes (or game turns).
    
*   •
    
    WR (Win Rate): Evaluates the percentage of matches won by a player.
    
    WR\=(NwinsNgames)×100%
    
    where Nwins is the number of matches won and Ngames is the total number of matches played.
    
*   •

    where Nground,succ is the number of successful grounding actions and Nground,att is the total number of grounding attempts.

### Appendix H Diplomacy

Diplomacy is a multiplayer strategy board game. Unlike traditional games, it relies heavily on negotiation, alliance-building, and backstabbing among players. Each player controls a country, and the objective is to expand territory through strategic positioning and diplomatic agreements. There are no random elements such as dice; all actions, including strategic maneuvers and alliances, are decided by the players and executed simultaneously. Success in Diplomacy is determined by strategic foresight, persuasion, and the ability to predict and outsmart opponents diplomatically.

In a standard game of Diplomacy, seven players each assume control of one of the Great Powers of early 20th-century Europe. Unlike games with asymmetric factions, these powers begin with a roughly equivalent number of home "supply centers" and a fixed set of army and fleet units, though their geographical starting positions present unique strategic challenges and opportunities. The game unfolds on a map of Europe divided into named provinces, some of which are designated as supply centers crucial for maintaining and building units. Throughout the game, which proceeds in biannual turns (Spring and Fall), players simultaneously submit written orders for their units to move, support other units (their own or allied), hold position, or convoy armies. The resolution of these orders determines territorial control and unit survival.

The highly interactive and competitive nature of Diplomacy, coupled with its emphasis on unstructured negotiation, makes it an ideal testbed for evaluating advanced decision-making agents. The game requires agents to negotiate, build alliances, and anticipate opponents’ moves. LLM-based agents need to make strategic decisions based on evolving alliances, manage long-term plans, and adapt quickly to the changing dynamics of the game.

\[Mukobi et al., [2023](#bib.bib111 "Welfare diplomacy: benchmarking language model cooperation")\] introduced Welfare Diplomacy, a general-sum variant of the traditional Diplomacy game, which balances strategic conquest and domestic welfare, providing a better framework for evaluating cooperative capabilities in multi-agent systems.

### Appendix I Werewolf

Werewolf is a multiplayer party game that revolves around social deduction, set in a fictional village where players are secretly assigned roles. Some players are werewolves, while others are villagers, and the goal is for the werewolves to eliminate the villagers or for the villagers to identify and eliminate the werewolves. The game is played in alternating night and day phases, where during the night, the werewolves secretly choose a villager to eliminate, and during the day, the players debate and vote on who they believe the werewolves are. The game requires careful observation, persuasion, and the ability to deceive or detect lies.

The most common setup is for 8 to 20 players, with a mix of werewolves, villagers, and special characters such as the seer, who can reveal a player’s true identity, or the doctor, who can protect a player from being eliminated. Players must use social deduction, communication, and strategy to identify who the werewolves are while avoiding being misled or eliminated. Success in Werewolf relies on the players’ ability to read social cues, form alliances, and strategically vote out potential werewolves while protecting key villagers.

As a highly interactive social deduction game, Werewolf\[Bailis et al., [2024a](#bib.bib34 "Werewolf arena: a case study in llm evaluation via social deduction")\] provides an ideal environment for evaluating agents that simulate social reasoning and decision-making. The game requires agents to make decisions based on limited information, assess the trustworthiness of other players, and adapt to shifting dynamics. LLM-based agents must manage interactions, detect deception, and navigate complex social situations to achieve their objectives.

Success in Stratego relies on strategic placement of pieces, anticipating the opponent’s moves, and managing both offensive and defensive strategies.

### Appendix K Limitations and Broad Impacts

#### K.1 Limitations

Despite the promising capabilities of DSGBench for evaluating LLM-based agents in complex strategic games, several limitations warrant acknowledgment. Firstly, the inherent complexity and extended duration of decision-making processes within these games necessitate long action sequences for meaningful evaluation. This can translate into substantial evaluation runtimes, potentially on the order of hours or days per trial, thereby posing significant computational demands and potentially limiting the scale of experimentation. Secondly, while DSGBench offers a curated set of evaluation scenarios, their current number and diversity are somewhat restricted. Consequently, the extent to which findings on agent capabilities can generalize to a broader spectrum of strategic challenges or entirely new game environments remains an open question requiring further investigation and an expansion of the benchmark suite. Finally, a significant current limitation is the absence of a readily available, large-scale, and standardized trajectory dataset spanning diverse strategic game scenarios within the benchmark. While individual games may have their own datasets, a unified collection designed for cross-game training and pre-training of LLM-based agents is lacking. The current lack of this unified resource hinders more rapid and standardized development of generalizable strategic reasoning capabilities in LLM-based agents.

#### K.2 Broad Impacts

DSGBench holds considerable potential to drive advancements in artificial intelligence by offering a more standardized and rigorous framework for the evaluation of strategic decision-making in LLM-based agents. By enabling systematic assessment across diverse and complex game environments, the benchmark can facilitate the identification of current agent limitations and guide targeted improvements, potentially leading to enhanced agent performance in intricate, dynamic settings. The insights gained from DSGBench evaluations may be applied in areas requiring advanced autonomous decision-making, such as advanced gaming AI, multi-robot coordination, and potentially certain types of autonomous systems operating in interactive environments. Furthermore, by encouraging the assessment of agents along multiple cognitive dimensions—spanning from long-term strategic planning to nuanced communicative competence. DSGBench could support the development of more robust and adaptable AI systems. While ambitious, a long-term aspiration is that transparent and reproducible evaluations through such benchmarks might contribute to discussions on fairness and accountability in AI decision-making processes.
