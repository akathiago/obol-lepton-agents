###### Abstract

Why is it that a ticking clock typically becomes less accurate when subject to outside noise but rarely the reverse? Here, we formalize this phenomenon by introducing _process causal asymmetry_ вҖ“ a fundamental difference in the amount of past information an autonomous agent must track to transform one stochastic process to another over an agent that transforms in the opposite direction. We then illustrate that this asymmetry can paradoxically be reversed when agents possess a quantum memory. Thus, the spontaneous direction in which processes get вҖҳsimplerвҖҷ may be different, depending on whether quantum information processing is allowed or not.

Suppose we observe two streams of data: the first, рқ’ң\=вҖҰвҒў010101вҒўвҖҰ, is an alternating stream of 0 and 1s, generated by a binary switch that flips each time-step; the second, в„¬\=вҖҰвҒў01230123вҒўвҖҰ, represents configurations of a revolving object that completes a full cycle every four time-steps. We are asked to decide between two possible causal explanations: (1) рқ’ң causes в„¬, such that each bt of в„¬ at time t is generated by some channel acting on at or (2) в„¬ causes рқ’ң such that the input and output of this channel are reversed. While we cannot conclusively rule out either causal explanation, the two causal structures are not symmetric. Let at and bt be the respective outputs of рқ’ң and в„¬ at time t. A simple channel channel enacting at\=btmod2 transforms в„¬ to рқ’ң. This channel is memoryless and time-independent. Any agent implementing it does not need to adapt its action to at or bt at times prior to t. In contrast, transforming from рқ’ң to в„¬ is more complex. An agent that sees at\=0 cannot decide whether bt\=0 or bt\=2 without at least 1 bit of information about the past (see Fig. [1](#S0.F1 "Figure 1 вҖЈ Causal Asymmetry of Classical and Quantum Autonomous Agents")).

In complexity science, each piece of information a machine must track is considered to be a necessary cause of future behaviourВ \[[1](#bib.bib1), [2](#bib.bib2)\]. As such, our thought experiment highlights a variant of _causal asymmetry_ - causal explanations in one direction appear more natural than the otherВ \[[3](#bib.bib3), [4](#bib.bib4), [5](#bib.bib5)\]. Here, рқ’ң can be thought of as a rudimentary discrete-time clock that tracks 1-bit of data about the current time, while в„¬ is a slightly more sophisticated clock that tracks 2\-bitsВ \[[6](#bib.bib6), [7](#bib.bib7)\]. Causal asymmetry then represents the intuition that it is easier to degrade a clock than to make it more accurate. Adding noise needs only a memoryless noise channel while reversing this requires adaptive operations.

The goal of this article is to (1) formalize the above intuitions using causal transducers вҖ“ a mathematical description of autonomous agents that transform one stochastic process to anotherВ \[[2](#bib.bib2)\] and (2) determine how quantum-enhanced agents can change what causal direction we consider to be more natural. This involves defining _process causal asymmetry_ as the difference in the amount of past data an agent must track to transform рқ’ң to в„¬ versus from в„¬ to рқ’ң; and examining how this difference changes when we deploy agents with quantum memoryВ \[[8](#bib.bib8)\]. We make a surprising observation: quantum processing can reverse causal asymmetry, such that рқ’ң causing в„¬ may be more natural when considering only classical agents, while the reverse is the more natural one when quantum agents are allowed.

Framework.вҖ” We adopt computational mechanics to describe stochastic processes and agents that transform them from one to anotherВ \[[9](#bib.bib9), [1](#bib.bib1), [2](#bib.bib2)\]. Formally, a stochastic process рқ’ң can be described by a bi-infinite sequence of random variables XвҶ”\=вҖҰвҒўXвҲ’1вҒўX0вҒўX1вҒўвҖҰ, where Xt denotes the probability the process emits xtвҲҲрқ’і at time t. Each instance of a process has a particular past xвҶҗ\=вҖҰвҒўxвҲ’1вҒўx0, and a conditional future governed by XвҶ’|xвҶҗ. Here we consider stationary stochastic processes, such that XвҶ” is time-translation invariant.

Autonomous agents are finite-state machines that can transform one stochastic process to anotherВ \[[2](#bib.bib2), [8](#bib.bib8)\]. At each time-step t, an agent accepts input xtвҲҲрқ’і and emits a corresponding output ytвҲҲрқ’ҙ. Taking the present as t\=0, the agent then experiences a history zвҶҗ\=(xвҶҗ,yвҶҗ). The operational behaviour of the agent is defined by an _input-output process_, a family of random variables YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ, which governs the probability the agent will emit yвҶ’\=y1вҒўy2вҒўвҖҰ when given future inputs xвҶ’\=x1вҒўx2вҒўвҖҰ. We say that an agent transforms рқ’ң to в„¬ when, given inputs sampled from рқ’ң, its corresponding outputs YвҶ” align with в„¬.

When YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ\=YвҶ’|xвҶ’ is history independent, the agent is _non-adaptive_. This represents the simplest input-output process. Agents implementing such transforms can be _memoryless_. They just need to apply the same transformation on xt at each time. The set of possible transforms, however, is extremely limited. There is no capability to generate temporal structure, such as converting a sequence of рқ’ң\=вҖҰвҒў0101вҒўвҖҰ to в„¬\=вҖҰвҒў01230123вҒўвҖҰ. To do this would require tracking some information about zвҶҗ. That is, the agent must possess an internal memory в„і that is configured in some state szвҶҗ\=kвҒў(zвҶҗ), such that there is a systematic method to sample from YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ for each possible xвҶ’ and zвҶҗ. Here, we assume that all our agents are causal, such that в„і does not contain any oracular information - information about the future that is not already contained in the pastВ \[[10](#bib.bib10), [11](#bib.bib11), [5](#bib.bib5)\].

To quantify the memory cost of the transformation, we make use of Пө\-transducers, the provably optimal classical agents for executing a given input-output processВ \[[2](#bib.bib2)\]. An Пө\-transducer operates on the rationale that an agent needs not distinguish two histories zвҶҗ and zвҶҗвҖІ if its future decisions never depend on this information, i.e. when YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ\=YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗвҖІ for all xвҶ’; which we abbreviate to the equivalence relation zвҶҗвҲјПөzвҶҗвҖІ. Thus, Пө\-transducer features an encoding function k such that kвҒў(zвҶҗ)\=kвҒў(zвҶҗвҖІ) if and only if zвҶҗвҲјПөzвҶҗвҖІ. The resulting memory states рқ’®\={si} are then in 1\-1 correspondence with the equivalence classes induced by вҲјПө. The dynamics of the input-output process can be realized by stochastic transitions between causal states, i.e., a set of transition matrices рқ’ҜвүЎ{T(y|x)}. Their elements TiвҒўj(y|x) then represent the probability an Пө\-transducer in state si will emit the symbol y upon receiving input x, and transition to state sj.

The memory states {si} are referred to as the causal states, owing to their interpretation as the minimal set of belief states an agent must hold to be able to execute YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ to perfect statistical fidelity. For each input stochastic process рқ’ң, the resulting agent memory will be driven to some stationary distribution, {ПҖi}, where ПҖi denotes the probability of being at causal state si when driven by input process рқ’ң. The resulting memory cost Cрқ’ң\=вҲ’вҲ‘ПҖiвҒўlog2вҒЎПҖi is then known as the statistical complexity of the process on input рқ’ң and is considered a fundamental measure of structure for YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ that characterizes how much past information any agent executing YвҶ’|xвҶ’,zвҶҗ must hold in memory. By minimizing the statistical complexity over all input-output processes that transform рқ’ң to в„¬, we obtain the minimal memory cost of the transformation, Cрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬.

Note also that this quantity can always be bounded above by CрқҹҺвҶ’в„¬, the memory cost of transforming a sequence of рқҹҺ\=вҖҰвҒў0000вҒўвҖҰ (or any other i.i.d sequence) to в„¬. This is because we can always choose to first erase рқ’ң using a memoryless channel at no cost. The resulting machine is known as the optimal predictive (or causal) model for в„¬В \[[1](#bib.bib1), [9](#bib.bib9), [5](#bib.bib5)\]. The states of such a machine are referred to as the causal states of в„¬. CрқҹҺвҶ’в„¬ is referred to as the statistical complexity of в„¬, representing the minimal information we need to store about its past to generate statistically correct future predictionsВ \[[1](#bib.bib1)\]. When CAвҶ’в„¬ saturates this bound, it suggests that tracking рқ’ң has no benefit for making predictions on в„¬.

Given two processes рқ’ң and в„¬, let Cрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬ denote the minimal past data needed to transform рқ’ң to в„¬, and Cв„¬вҶ’рқ’ң as the equivalent when transforming from в„¬ to рқ’ң. Then, we define _process causal asymmetry_ by the difference

О”CвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬)\=Cрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬вҲ’Cв„¬вҶ’рқ’ң.

(1)

A positive О”CвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬) indicates that more memory is required to transform рқ’ң to в„¬ than from в„¬ to рқ’ң. Our opening example illustrates this, with Cрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬\=1 and Cв„¬вҶ’рқ’ң\=0, such that О”CвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬)\=1.

So far, we assumed that в„і stores only classical states. However, quantum agents encoding each causal state si to corresponding quantum state |siвҹ© exhibit a memory advantageВ \[[12](#bib.bib12), [8](#bib.bib8)\]. The quantum causal states can always be taken to be pure states \[[8](#bib.bib8)\], and the quantum complexity is defined as the von Neumann entropy Qрқ’ң\=SвҒў(ПҒрқ’ң)\=вҲ’trвҒў(ПҒрқ’ңвҒўlogвҒЎПҒрқ’ң) of the average state of the quantum memory, ПҒрқ’ң\=вҲ‘iПҖрқ’ң,iвҒў|siвҹ©вҒўвҹЁsi|, where |siвҹ©вҒўвҹЁsi| is the quantum state representing the classical causal state si. A quantum model is at least as efficient as the best classical model, i.e., Qрқ’ңвүӨCрқ’ң \[[12](#bib.bib12), [8](#bib.bib8)\]. By minimizing the quantum complexity over all input-output processes that transform рқ’ң to в„¬, we obtain Qрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬, the minimal memory needed by quantum agents to transform рқ’ң to в„¬. For certain рқ’ң and в„¬, we have that Qрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬<Cрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬. In analogy to the classical case, we define process causal asymmetry for quantum agents as

О”QвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬)\=Qрқ’ңвҶ’в„¬вҲ’Qв„¬вҶ’рқ’ң.

_Main Results.вҖ”_ We now state our main results. The first shows that the direction of causal asymmetry can be reversed by quantum agents.

###### Result 1 (Inconsistent Causal Asymmetry).

There exist processes рқ’ң and в„¬ with process causal asymmetries simultaneously obeying О”CвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬)\>0 and О”QвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬)<0, which implies that the classical memory resources necessary to map рқ’ң to в„¬ are higher than that of mapping в„¬ to рқ’ң, while quantum mechanically it is the other way around. Causal asymmetry can thus reverse depending on whether quantum agents are allowed or not.

Our second result shows that not only an inconsistent causal asymmetry exists, but the magnitude of this inconsistency can be arbitrarily large.

###### Result 2 (Unbounded Inconsistent Causal Asymmetry).

There exist processes рқ’ң and в„¬n, for nвҲҲв„• such that the classical process asymmetry О”CвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬) diverges with increasing n, while the quantum one, О”QвҒў(рқ’ң,в„¬), remains bounded but with the opposite sign.

Our results thus illustrate - for the first time to our knowledge - that quantum and classical theories can result in different conclusions about the most natural order of causal transformations. The possibility of storing information on non-orthogonal quantum states, not only leads to a quantitative reduction of memory cost \[[12](#bib.bib12), [8](#bib.bib8)\], but to a qualitatively different behaviour in the direction of causation.

Discussion.вҖ” Transforming one stochastic process into another generally requires memory. Here, we introduced _process causal asymmetry_ to investigate the difference in memory costs required for transformations between a pair of stochastic processes рқ’ң and в„¬. We demonstrated that agents with quantum memory exhibit dramatically different behaviour from those with classical memory. In particular, we provided the first explicit example where the direction of causation is flipped between classical and quantum models, and the gap grows unboundedly with system size.

There are several natural directions for further research. The first involves linking our work to that of causal asymmetry in the context of causal vs. retrocausal models вҖ“ where memory costs of modeling stochastic processes in forward-time vs. reverse-time can differВ \[[3](#bib.bib3)\]. Here, quantum models could remove this asymmetry, but a full reversal remains unknownВ \[[5](#bib.bib5)\]. Meanwhile, our quantum agents transform classical data to classical data - while quantum agents (combs) that transform quantum processes also existВ \[[14](#bib.bib14), [15](#bib.bib15), [16](#bib.bib16)\]. It would be exciting to unify such settings, considering causal asymmetry when both time and input-outputs are reversed in contexts of both classical and quantum outputs.

Conceptually, process causal asymmetry also hints at some underlying resource theory on temporal correlations. For example, a sequence of i.i.d. random variables would take no memory to generate, while transforming this to a highly non-Markovian process requires adaptive agents with increasing memory resourcesВ \[[17](#bib.bib17), [18](#bib.bib18)\]. This opens an interesting research direction towards constructing a formal hierarchy of non-adaptive agents and relating it to existing ideas from resource theories of non-MarkovianityВ \[[19](#bib.bib19), [20](#bib.bib20)\]. Indeed, the power of memory and adaptive operations has already been recognized in quantum state preparationВ \[[21](#bib.bib21), [22](#bib.bib22)\], gate synthesisВ \[[23](#bib.bib23), [24](#bib.bib24)\], exhibiting contextualityВ \[[11](#bib.bib11), [25](#bib.bib25), [26](#bib.bib26), [27](#bib.bib27)\], and generating data-stringsВ \[[18](#bib.bib18)\] as well as being a key differentiator between quantum error mitigation and error correctionВ \[[28](#bib.bib28)\]. Therefore, this direction could provide valuable insights on when quantum agents have greatest advantage and where they are most useful.
